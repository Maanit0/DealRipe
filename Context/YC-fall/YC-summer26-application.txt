What is your company going to make? Please describe your product and what it does or will do.
DealRipe is the deal inspection layer that sits underneath a B2B sales team's existing stack (CRM, call recording tools, email, calendar). For sales teams with 10+ reps, it does three things:
1. First, it audits every active deal against the team's own sales qualification framework (SCOTSMAN, MEDDIC, MEDDPICC, whatever they use) and surfaces what's missing. For example, budget not confirmed, economic buyer not identified, or procurement timeline not mapped. The blindspots sales reps either bias optimistically or forget to log or ask about on calls.
2. Second, before every customer call, it tells the rep the exact situational questions to ask to close those gaps. Reps stop walking out of calls without confirming budget. CROs stop spending 10 hours a week manually verifying deals.
3. Third, after every call, it pulls the actual answers out of the transcript and writes them back into the CRM with the customer's verbatim quote as evidence. When a CRO inspects a deal, they see "Budget confirmed at $200,000, Q3 procurement cycle" with the exact 12 seconds from the call where the prospect said it. The data going into the CRM is what the customer actually said, not what the rep typed three days later from memory.

Today, the MVP runs the audit and question generation against ingested call transcripts and ships extractions to a dashboard. I've demoed it on 8 CRO calls. The next two builds are the Salesforce write-back integration for Mike Rogers's pilot at Mind.io (target: week of May 11) and the Microsoft Teams transcript pipeline for Mark Buman's pilot at Magaya (target: post legal review and code review on May 11).

Why CROs want this: every other AI sales tool (Gong, Clari, Salesforce Einstein) reads from the CRM and trusts what's there. Sales reps control CRM input and their incentives reward making pipelines look healthier than they are. Every AI in the stack confidently summarizes data the rep fabricated. We sit underneath those tools and fix the data itself, so everything above us actually means something.

Why did you pick this idea to work on? Do you have domain expertise in this area? How do you know people need what you’re making?
Why this idea?

Sales is the only function in a company where the work product is a conversation. I learned that when running my marketing agency at 19. I lived in cold outreach, discovery calls, and the pain of qualifying a deal before I'd done enough enterprise sales coursework to know what qualifying meant. The reps and CROs I'm building for now are running the same loops I ran, just at a scale where the loop runs ten thousand times a quarter and the CRO can't see most of them. That gap between "what the rep said happened" and "what the customer actually said" is the problem I want to spend ten years on.

I picked DealRipe after fifteen discovery calls with CROs and VPs of Sales between March 23 and early April. The same pattern surfaced unprompted across most of them: reps have happy ears, they bias deal data optimistically or forget to log it, qualification fields stay manual and stale, and by the time a CRO realizes a deal isn't real, weeks of pipeline value are gone. As teams scale past ten reps, forecast accuracy drops sharply because the qualification gaps compound. CROs spend their one-on-ones with reps gathering data when those calls should be about unblocking deals. Gong has the recordings. The time to listen to them doesn't exist. No CRO has 40 hours a quarter to listen to 100 calls. DealRipe is the layer that does the listening for them. What sealed it: three independent CROs (Mike Rogers at Mind.io, Joe Dillon at Cayosoft, Kevin Brooks at Procurant) named close-date prediction as the single AI output they'd pay for, in three separate calls. Mike Rogers built a v0 of DealRipe himself in 24 hours using Claude before our second call. He demoed his version to me. He's still buying mine because his runs on his laptop and won't scale to his team.

Domain expertise
I sold B2B before I started DealRipe. On a gap year after my freshman year at Berkeley, I built a marketing agency for medical spas to $11,558 peak MRR ($138K ARR) in six months at age 19, closing 90-day contracts at $1,500 to $2,000 per month. I sent 100+ cold emails a week, ran my own discovery, and learned where deals die: usually because I hadn't qualified budget, pain, authority, or timeline early enough. The same root cause that killed my deals at 19 is what CROs are paying their reps to surface today. I've been on both sides of the qualification framework: the rep trying to look better than the deal is, and now the founder building the tool that catches it.

How do I know people need this?
Three pilot commitments in eight demos. Nine more CROs mid-funnel. And one CRO who built the same product himself before deciding to buy mine.
Who are your competitors? What do you understand about your business that they don’t?
The most-named competitor in my demos is Gong. Every CRO I talk to is either using Gong or has used it. Gong is reactive and post-call. It transcribes, summarizes, and lets a CRO ask questions of the recording after the meeting. That's useful, but it inherits the same root problem every AI sales tool inherits. If the rep didn't ask the right qualification question on the call, no amount of post-call summary recovers the answer. Gong is summarizing data the rep failed to surface.

DealRipe sits one step upstream. Before the rep takes the call, the agent reads the team's qualification framework (SCOTSMAN, MEDDIC, MEDDPICC, or a custom one), reads the deal's history, identifies the gaps, and generates the three situational questions the rep needs to ask on this specific deal to unblock it. After the call, the agent extracts the answers from the transcript with verbatim quote evidence and writes them back to the CRM. The CRO inspects deals against fields that came from the customer's words, not the rep's interpretation.

Gong gives a CRO 100 recordings to listen to. DealRipe gives them the answer. The other comparisons that come up are Clari (forecasting, downstream of where I sit) and Salesforce Einstein (the native AI layer). Both consume CRM data and trust what's there. I don't.

The competitor most CROs underestimate is themselves. Three of the CROs I've demoed to have built or are building a version of DealRipe internally using Claude or Perplexity. Mike Rogers at Mind.io built his version in 24 hours and demoed it to me on the call. He's still buying mine. The reason is everything around the prompt: multi-tenant deployment, framework configurability across teams, Salesforce write-back, and a system the CRO doesn't have to maintain on his laptop.

Market and the path:
Gong does $300 million in ARR across 5,000 customers and was valued at $7.25 billion at peak, $4.5 billion in current secondary trading. Clari is at $2.6 billion. Outreach is at $4.4 billion. Revenue intelligence is large enough to support multiple multi-billion-dollar outcomes, and that's before agentic AI changes what a "rep" looks like. DealRipe sits one layer upstream of Gong. Our market is every B2B SaaS company with ten or more reps and multiple sales managers, which is roughly 30,000 companies globally. At an average $35,000 ACV (the midpoint between a 10-rep team and a 30-rep team at our pricing), capturing just 3% of this market produces $30M ARR. 10% capture produces $100M ARR, which is roughly Gong's customer count today. Gong did $300M ARR in fiscal 2025 and traded at $7.25B at peak. We do not need to dominate the category to be a billion-dollar outcome. We need to be one layer of the revenue intelligence stack that CROs trust and pay for.

Why this expands
Every closed-won and closed-lost deal that flows through DealRipe makes the next deal's qualification scoring smarter. Once a CRO trusts the input data DealRipe surfaces, the natural expansion is into adjacent sales functions: rep ramp, deal-level forecasting (which we currently feed but don't own), territory planning, and rep performance benchmarking. What we sell first is a qualification audit. What we become is the place a company keeps its real deal data.
